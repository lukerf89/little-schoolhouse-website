Curriculum Planning Made Simple

Finding the right educational path for your family

Every family is unique, and so is every child's learning journey. Let's explore curriculum options that honor your family's values, schedule, and your children's individual needs.

Starting Your Curriculum Journey

Choosing curriculum can feel overwhelming at first. Store shelves lined with workbooks, online programs with endless options, and well-meaning advice from every direction. We've been there, and we want to help simplify this process for you.

"The beautiful thing about homeschooling is that you don't have to get it perfect the first time. You can adjust, adapt, and change as you learn what works for your family." - Amanda Freeman

After years of trial and error, we've learned that the best curriculum is the one that:

  • Your children actually engage with
  • Fits your family's daily rhythm
  • Aligns with your educational goals
  • You can afford and sustain
  • Brings joy to your homeschool

Popular Homeschool Approaches

Understanding different educational philosophies can help you find what resonates with your family:

Traditional/School-at-Home

What it is: Structured learning with textbooks, workbooks, and tests similar to traditional school.

Best for: Families who prefer clear structure and measurable progress.

Popular curricula: Abeka, Bob Jones, Saxon Math

Classical Education

What it is: Based on the trivium (grammar, logic, rhetoric) with emphasis on great books and classical languages.

Best for: Families valuing rigorous academics and classical literature.

Popular curricula: Classical Conversations, Well-Trained Mind

Charlotte Mason

What it is: Living books, nature study, short lessons, and habit training.

Best for: Families wanting a gentle, literature-rich approach.

Popular curricula: Ambleside Online, Simply Charlotte Mason

Unit Studies

What it is: All subjects integrated around a central theme or topic.

Best for: Multi-age families and hands-on learners.

Popular curricula: KONOS, Five in a Row, Unit Studies by Amanda Bennett

Unschooling/Interest-Led

What it is: Child-directed learning based on natural interests and real-life experiences.

Best for: Self-motivated learners and flexible families.

Resources: Local libraries, community resources, online platforms

Eclectic/Mixed Approach

What it is: Combining elements from various methods to create a custom approach.

Best for: Families who want flexibility to adapt to each child.

Example: Classical math, Charlotte Mason literature, unit study science

Planning by Grade Level

Elementary Years (K-5)

Focus: Building foundations in reading, writing, and arithmetic while nurturing curiosity.

  • Reading: Phonics programs like All About Reading or Teach Your Child to Read in 100 Easy Lessons
  • Math: Math-U-See, Singapore Math, or Right Start Mathematics
  • Science: Nature walks, simple experiments, Apologia Elementary Science
  • History/Social Studies: Story of the World, Beautiful Feet Books

Daily rhythm: 2-3 hours of formal learning with plenty of play and exploration

Middle School (6-8)

Focus: Developing independent learning skills and deeper subject exploration.

  • Language Arts: IEW writing programs, literature studies
  • Math: Teaching Textbooks, VideoText, Beast Academy
  • Science: Apologia, Elemental Science, or real-life projects
  • History: Mystery of History, Notgrass History

Daily rhythm: 3-4 hours with increasing independent work

High School (9-12)

Focus: College preparation, career exploration, and life skills.

  • Core Subjects: Consider dual enrollment at local community colleges
  • Online Options: Khan Academy, Coursera, local virtual schools
  • Transcripts: Keep detailed records for college applications
  • Testing: Plan for SAT/ACT, consider CLEP exams for college credit

Daily rhythm: 4-6 hours including independent study and projects

Homeschooling on a Budget

Quality education doesn't have to break the bank. Here are proven strategies for affordable homeschooling:

Free Resources

  • Khan Academy: Complete K-12 curriculum
  • Easy Peasy All-in-One: Full curriculum, all grades
  • Ambleside Online: Charlotte Mason curriculum
  • CK-12: STEM resources and textbooks
  • Your library: Books, DVDs, online resources, programs

Money-Saving Tips

  • Buy used curriculum at homeschool conventions
  • Join curriculum swap groups on Facebook
  • Share resources with other homeschool families
  • Use the library extensively
  • Take advantage of free museum days
  • Apply for Arkansas LEARNS Act funding

Arkansas LEARNS Act Funding

Remember, Arkansas families may qualify for Education Freedom Accounts through the LEARNS Act. These funds can be used for:

  • Curriculum and educational materials
  • Tutoring services
  • Online learning programs
  • Educational therapy services
  • Testing fees

Practical Planning Tools

Creating Your Daily Schedule

A flexible routine (not a rigid schedule) helps everyone know what to expect:

Sample Morning Routine:

  • 8:00 - Breakfast and morning chores
  • 9:00 - Bible/Character study (15 min)
  • 9:15 - Math (30-45 min)
  • 10:00 - Language Arts (30-45 min)
  • 10:45 - Snack and movement break
  • 11:00 - History/Science/Unit Study
  • 12:00 - Lunch and free time

Afternoon options: Art projects, nature walks, library visits, co-op classes, sports, or interest-led learning.

Year-Round vs. Traditional Schedule

Many Arkansas families find year-round schooling works well:

  • 6 weeks on, 1 week off - Prevents burnout, allows for regular breaks
  • 4-day school weeks - Fridays for field trips, co-op, or catch-up
  • Seasonal adjustments - Lighter schedule during harvest or planting seasons
  • Traditional schedule - Following public school calendar for simplicity

Overcoming Common Challenges

"My child hates math!"

Try a different approach: If workbooks aren't working, consider manipulatives (Math-U-See), games (Right Start), or online programs (Beast Academy). Sometimes a break and fresh start makes all the difference.

"We're always behind!"

Remember: You're not behind; you're exactly where you need to be. Focus on mastery over pace. Your child's education is a marathon, not a sprint.

"Teaching multiple ages is hard!"

Combine subjects when possible (history, science, read-alouds). Use independent work for older children while teaching younger ones. Consider unit studies that engage all ages.

"I don't feel qualified!"

You don't need to know everything. Learn alongside your children. Use teacher's guides, video lessons, and co-op classes for subjects outside your comfort zone.
